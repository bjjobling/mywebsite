A practical matter

For a while I owned a sailboat, and every sailor wants to know how fast their boat can go. The two things a sailor must know to go fast are the strength and relative direction of the wind. This information is provided to newer boat owners via a designer's performance chart known as a polar diagram. The diagram would provide a potential hull speed over a range of wind speeds and relative wind angles.

Like many boats of its era, my 1977 Tartan 37, Island Pearl, did not come with such a chart. However, over the years various Tartan owners had gathered performance data on their own boats and shared with other owners on the forums. Some had submitted it to the Offshore Racing Congress for inclusion in their sailboat performance database.

In 2020 I imported variously-sourced Tartan 37 data into an excel spreadsheet, along with that of it's European stablemate the Condor 37, and produced polar charts which I then uploaded to the Tartan 37 Owner's forum.

A copy of my original spreadsheet can be found here.

The challenge

To reproduce the polar charts I produced in Excel on the web page using JavaScript tools and .xml data imports. As I have a sum total of three days' reading on html and JavaScript this will be a 'proof of concept' demonstration rather than 'best practices'.

Limitations

One of the limitations I may already face is that the polar chart is not (or was not) native to Excel, and is a modified 'donut' chart, with polar coordinates (r, Î¸) converted to Cartesian (x, y) coordinates.

Course of action #1

Both Plotly and Chart.js offer polar chart solutions, but so far neither seems to offer a true polar coordinate chart solution; rather, conversion to Cartesian coordinates may be required.

COA #2

Project a Cartesian scatter or line chart onto a polar/radar background image. This may be the simplest and most practical solution. Html allows for precise positioning and plotting of images on a page; scaling for different devices will be in order.
